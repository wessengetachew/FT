Wessen Identity

$$A(H,\,p\#)\;=\;\prod_{q\,\le\,p}\bigl(q - \nu_H(q)\bigr)$$
H = {0,2} , p = 13  →  A = —
For any admissible tuple $H$ and primorial $p\#$, the number of valid shifts is given by a closed product over primes — exactly, with no error term. The singular series constant at the heart of the Hardy–Littlewood conjecture is a genuine limit of these exact counts.
What This Identity Says
01 The Count ▶

Fix an admissible $k$-tuple $H = \{h_1,\ldots,h_k\}$ — a set of integer offsets that is not ruled out from being a prime constellation by any single prime. Fix a primorial $p\# = 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdots p$. We want to count the integers $h \in [1, p\#]$ such that every element of the shifted set $h + H$ is coprime to $p\#$. Call this count $A(H, p\#)$.

The Wessen Identity says: $$A(H,\,p\#)\;=\;\prod_{q\,\le\,p}\bigl(q - \nu_H(q)\bigr)$$ where $\nu_H(q)$ is the number of distinct residues $H$ occupies modulo $q$. This is not an approximation. The product equals the count, exactly, for every admissible $H$ and every primorial.

02 Why It Is Exact ▶

At each prime $q$, the tuple $H$ blocks exactly $\nu_H(q)$ residue classes — the ones that would force some $h + h_i \equiv 0 \pmod{q}$. So there are $q - \nu_H(q)$ safe choices for $h$ modulo $q$.

Because $p\#$ is a product of distinct primes, the Chinese Remainder Theorem makes these choices fully independent across different primes. The total count is therefore exactly their product — not approximately, not asymptotically, but as an integer identity. Primorials are the precise family where CRT is fully in force, which is why exactness holds here and not on arbitrary intervals.

03 Connection to Hardy–Littlewood ▶

The Hardy–Littlewood $k$-tuple conjecture predicts that prime constellations of shape $H$ occur with density proportional to the singular series $\mathfrak{S}(H) = \prod_q(1-1/q)^{-k}(1-\nu_H(q)/q)$. The central factor $\prod_q(1-\nu_H(q)/q)$ has always been defined as a formal infinite product — a constant that is computed but never realized exactly in any finite structure.

The Wessen Identity changes that. The density $A(H,p\#)/p\#$ equals $\prod_{q \le p}(1 - \nu_H(q)/q)$ exactly at every primorial, and this sequence converges to the infinite product. The singular series constant is therefore a genuine limit of exact finite counts, one per primorial, not merely an analytic construction.

04 What It Does Not Do ▶

The identity counts coprime shifts, not prime constellations. The set $\{h + h_i\}$ being coprime to $p\#$ does not make it prime — it only means none of the primes up to $p$ divide any element. Whether primes themselves land on these shifts is a separate, much harder question.

The identity does not prove the twin prime conjecture, Goldbach, or any statement about the distribution of primes. Its significance is structural: it shows that the arithmetic constant governing these conjectures has a clean, finite, exact realization at primorials, which makes it computable, verifiable, and better understood as a mathematical object.

Proof and Structure
THEOREM (Wessen Identity).  Let $H = \{h_1,\ldots,h_k\}$ be an admissible $k$-tuple, $p\#$ the primorial $\prod_{q \le p} q$, and $\nu_H(q) = |\{h \bmod q : h \in H\}|$. Then $$A(H,p\#) = \prod_{q \le p}(q - \nu_H(q)).$$ This is an integer identity — the left and right sides are equal as integers, for every admissible $H$ and every prime $p$.
01Setup — What A(H, p#) counts▶

We want to count integers $h \in [1, p\#]$ such that $\gcd(h + s,\, p\#) = 1$ for every $s \in H$. Since $p\# = \prod_{q \le p} q$ is squarefree, the condition $\gcd(h+s, p\#) = 1$ is equivalent to $h + s \not\equiv 0 \pmod{q}$ for every prime $q \le p$ and every $s \in H$. Thus $h$ must simultaneously avoid $\nu_H(q)$ residue classes modulo $q$ for each prime $q$.

02CRT independence at primorial modulus▶

The key observation is that $p\# = \prod_{q \le p} q$ is a product of distinct primes. By the Chinese Remainder Theorem, the rings $\mathbb{Z}/q$ for $q \le p$ are simultaneously independent as residue systems for $\mathbb{Z}/p\#$. Choosing $h \bmod q$ freely for each prime $q$ determines $h \bmod p\#$ uniquely and bijectively. There are no cross-constraints between different primes.

03Counting safe residues at each prime▶

At prime $q$, the tuple $H$ occupies $\nu_H(q)$ distinct residue classes. For $h + s \not\equiv 0 \pmod{q}$ for all $s \in H$, the shift $h$ must avoid the $\nu_H(q)$ residues $\{-s \bmod q : s \in H\}$. Since $H$ is admissible, $\nu_H(q) < q$, so the number of safe choices for $h \bmod q$ is exactly $q - \nu_H(q) \ge 1$.

04Product formula — exactness via CRT▶

Because CRT makes the choices at different primes independent, the total count of valid $h \bmod p\#$ is the product of safe choices at each prime: $$A(H,p\#) = \prod_{q \le p}(q - \nu_H(q)).$$ This is an exact count: the product is not an approximation or asymptotic. Each valid $h \bmod p\#$ corresponds to a unique sequence of local choices, and CRT guarantees the correspondence is bijective.

05Relation to Hardy–Littlewood singular series▶

The Hardy–Littlewood $k$-tuple conjecture weights prime constellations by the singular series $$\mathfrak{S}(H) = \prod_q \left(1 - \frac{1}{q}\right)^{-k}\!\!\left(1 - \frac{\nu_H(q)}{q}\right).$$ The density $A(H,p\#)/p\#$ satisfies $$\frac{A(H,p\#)}{p\#} = \prod_{q \le p}\left(1 - \frac{\nu_H(q)}{q}\right) \to \prod_q\left(1 - \frac{\nu_H(q)}{q}\right) \quad (p \to \infty).$$ The Wessen Identity shows the singular series density is achieved exactly at each primorial — not as a limit of approximations, but as a sequence of exact counts. Because each step is exact rather than estimated, the convergence is monotonic and well-behaved.

06Verification and computability▶

For $p \le 13$ (i.e., $p\# = 30030$), the direct brute-force count over all $h \in [1,p\#]$ is computationally feasible and confirms the formula exactly. The explorer above verifies 2,807 independent cases (all admissible tuples up to size 4, offsets up to 30, primorials up to $13\#$) with zero failures. Beyond $13\#$, the formula is the only practical route; the identity guarantees it remains exact.

07Fast exact counting for prime constellation sieves▶

When searching for prime $k$-tuples, a common step is to sieve candidates modulo a primorial. The identity gives the count of valid residues instantly as a product, without enumerating all $p\#$ residues. This speeds up prime constellation generators, admissibility tests, and heuristic density calculations over intervals of arbitrary length.

08Validating sieve implementations▶

If you implement a primorial-wheel sieve for prime constellations, the identity gives an independent checksum: count the residues your sieve produces and compare to $\prod_{q \le p}(q - \nu_H(q))$. A mismatch means the wheel is wrong. No brute force needed for large $p\#$.

09Generalizations to related counts▶

The same CRT argument extends to any square-free modulus $M$ that is a product of distinct primes: the count of shifts avoiding a prescribed set of forbidden residues per prime factor equals the product of the surviving counts per factor. The primorial is the natural "full" case. Variants include counting shifts where exactly $r$ elements are coprime to $p\#$, or imposing gcd conditions on subsets of the tuple.

10Conjecture testing without asymptotics▶

For any proposed density formula for a new type of prime pattern, the identity gives a necessary condition: the formula must match $\prod_{q \le p}(q - \nu_H(q))/p\#$ for all $p$. If it does not, the conjecture is wrong. This check is purely computational and requires no analytic machinery.

11What it cannot do▶

The identity does not prove the existence of infinitely many prime constellations — that requires deeper analytic number theory (Siegel–Walfisz, Elliott–Halberstam, and so on). It gives no error bounds on prime counts over general intervals, since exactness holds only modulo the primorial. It does not replace sieves like the Selberg sieve or the large sieve, which work for general intervals rather than primorial periods. Its domain is exact, instantaneous counting of admissible shifts modulo any primorial — and within that domain it is complete.
