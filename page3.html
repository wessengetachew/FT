The Saturation Jump Theorem

D(t) = ∏p (1 − min(t+1, p) / p²)
jump in d(log D)/dt at t = p−1  =  1 / p(p−1)

The block-coprime density D(n) decays smoothly between events, then drops sharply at each prime threshold. The jump sizes are exact, encode every prime, and are recoverable from the derivative alone.

§1Statement & Proof
Let D(t) = ∏p prime (1 − min(t+1, p) / p²) for real t ≥ 0. This function is continuous, piecewise smooth, with a kink at each integer t = p−1 for every prime p. The left-derivative and right-derivative exist at every point, and at each saturation threshold: (d/dt log D)|t=p−1, left − (d/dt log D)|t=p−1, right  =  1 / p(p−1) Equivalently: prime p contributes a log-derivative kink of exactly 1/p(p−1) at t = p−1, and zero contribution everywhere else. The kink sizes {1/2, 1/6, 1/12, 1/20, 1/30, 1/42, …} uniquely determine the prime sequence.
1
Structure of the product. Each prime p contributes one factor to D(t). For t < p−1 (prime p is "active"): factorp(t) = 1 − (t+1)/p²    [linear in t, slope −1/p²] For t ≥ p−1 (prime p is "saturated"): factorp(t) = 1 − p/p² = (p−1)/p    [constant] The factor is continuous at t = p−1 (both expressions equal (p−1)/p there). So D(t) itself is continuous everywhere, only its derivative has jumps.
2
Log-derivative of a single active factor. For t < p−1: d/dt log factorp(t) = −(1/p²) / (1 − (t+1)/p²) Evaluating at t → p−1 from the left (so t+1 → p): = −(1/p²) / (1 − p/p²) = −(1/p²) / ((p−1)/p) = −1 / p(p−1)
3
Log-derivative of a saturated factor. For t ≥ p−1, factorp is the constant (p−1)/p, so: d/dt log factorp(t) = 0
4
The jump at t = p−1. The contribution of prime p to d(log D)/dt transitions from −1/p(p−1) (left) to 0 (right). The jump (right minus left) is: 0 − (−1/p(p−1)) = +1/p(p−1) This is exact. No approximation. All other primes q ≠ p contribute smoothly at t = p−1 (q is either already saturated or still strictly active), so their contributions to d(log D)/dt are continuous there. ∎
5
Prime recovery. Given a jump size s = 1/p(p−1), recover p by solving p² − p − 1/s = 0: p = (1 + √(1 + 4/s)) / 2 This gives an exact integer for every valid jump size, and that integer is prime by construction. The map p ↦ 1/p(p−1) is strictly decreasing on primes, so the bijection is unambiguous: the multiset of jump sizes encodes the primes exactly.

§5Between Events — The Smooth Decay

Between consecutive saturation events n = q−1 and n = p−1 (consecutive primes q < p), D(t) decays smoothly. The log-derivative has no jumps in this interval; it is driven only by the active primes r > t+1:

d/dt log D(t) = − Σp active (1/p²) / (1 − (t+1)/p²)

Open Question

The jump structure is complete: every prime p contributes exactly 1/p(p−1) to the log-derivative at t = p−1. But between jumps, the smooth decay depends on the full set of active primes. Does the rate of smooth decay between n = q−1 and n = p−1 encode anything about the prime gap p − q?

The active primes during this interval are exactly those with r > n+1, so their local factors are still varying. The curvature of log D in this interval is determined by the distribution of primes beyond the current threshold. If prime gaps are large, fewer primes contribute actively, and the smooth decay is slower. Whether this can be made precise — whether the decay rate in a given interval gives a lower bound on the next prime gap — is open.

A related question: the smooth parts of D(t) lie between a sequence of kinks. The kink sizes are 1/p(p−1) ≈ 1/p² for large p. The smooth decay between them converges to 0 as n → ∞ at rate governed by Mertens' theorem. What is the precise relationship between the smooth decay law and the distribution of gaps in the prime sequence?
