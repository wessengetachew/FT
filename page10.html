A Continuous Generalization of the Feller–Tornier Constant
via Saturated Prime Partitions and the Wrench Method

Eliminating prime products from the block-coprime density $C(n)$ via the classical Wrench technique (prime zeta function + Möbius inversion to Riemann zeta values)
Author: Wessen Getachew  ·  June 2026
Abstract We present a generalized framework for evaluating the arithmetic density of $n+1$ consecutive integers checked for mutual coprimality. The Feller–Tornier constant $C_{FT}$ (its $n=1$ member) and related sieve constants have historically been treated as isolated infinite products over primes. We introduce a dynamic parameter $n$ that partitions the prime universe into a saturated domain ($p\le n+1$) and an active domain ($p>n+1$), separating global geometric scaling via $\zeta(2)$ from local sifting constraints $D(n)$. Using the classical Wrench technique — logarithm–Maclaurin expansion, the prime zeta function $P(s)$, and its Möbius inversion $P(s)=\sum_m \tfrac{\mu(m)}{m}\ln\zeta(ms)$ (Wrench 1961) — the active tail $\Omega(n)$ is rewritten as a convergent series of classical Riemann zeta values, eliminating all prime-product dependence. The result is verified numerically against direct sieve computation for $n=1$.
§1Introduction & Historical Context ▸
  • The baseline grid. At $n=0$, density maps to $C(0)=1$ and raw density $D(0)=\dfrac{1}{\zeta(2)}=\dfrac{6}{\pi^2}$.
  • The Feller–Tornier core. At $n=1$: $D(1)=\prod_p\left(1-\dfrac{2}{p^2}\right)\approx 0.322634$, with classical variant $C_{FT}=\dfrac{1+D(1)}{2}\approx 0.661317$.
  • The problem of isolation. The Feller–Tornier constant and related sieve constants have traditionally been computed one value at a time. The contribution here is organizational: exhibiting them as integer samples $C(n)$ of one continuous family governed by block length $n$ — the same $n$ that drives the depth slider across Pages 4, 6, 8, and 9. The computational method (prime zeta + Möbius inversion) is the classical Wrench technique, not new here.
§2Theoretical Framework & the Saturated–Active Partition ▸
  • Block length. Formulate the condition for checking $n+1$ consecutive integers for mutual coprimality.
  • Density relation.DEF
    $$D(n)=\frac{C(n)}{\zeta(2)}$$
  • The prime state machine. Threshold condition $p\le n+1$:
    • Saturated domain ($p\le n+1$): rigid factor $\left(1-\dfrac1p\right)$ — already at maximum block depth.
    • Active domain ($p>n+1$): dynamic factor $\left(1-\dfrac{n+1}{p^2}\right)$ — scaling with block size.
§3Derivation of the Master Density Function ▸
  • Product decomposition.
    $$D(n)=\left[\prod_{p\le n+1}\left(1-\frac1p\right)\right]\cdot\Omega(n)$$
  • Active tail.DEF
    $$\Omega(n)=\prod_{p>n+1}\left(1-\frac{n+1}{p^2}\right)$$
    • §4Algebraic Isolation of $\Omega(n)$ — Elimination of Prime Products ▸
  • Logarithmic transformation.
    $$\ln\Omega(n)=\sum_{p>n+1}\ln\left(1-\frac{n+1}{p^2}\right)$$
  • Maclaurin expansion with $\ln(1-x)=-\sum_{k=1}^\infty \dfrac{x^k}{k}$, $x=\dfrac{n+1}{p^2}$:
    $$\ln\Omega(n)=-\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{(n+1)^k}{k}\sum_{p>n+1}\frac{1}{p^{2k}}$$
  • Truncated prime zeta function.DEF $P_{>n+1}(s)=P(s)-\sum_{p\le n+1}p^{-s}$:
    $$\Omega(n)=\exp\left(-\sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{(n+1)^k}{k}\,P_{>n+1}(2k)\right)$$
  • Möbius inversion to Riemann zeta space. The exact dictionary mapping prime zeta to classical zeta:
    $$P(2k)=\sum_{m=1}^\infty \frac{\mu(m)}{m}\ln\zeta(2km)$$
    At this point no prime appears explicitly anywhere in the closed form — $\Omega(n)$, and hence $C(n)$, is expressed purely through Riemann zeta values $\zeta(4),\zeta(6),\zeta(8),\dots$
  • LEMMA As $k\to\infty$, $\ln\zeta(2km)\to 0$ exponentially fast, guaranteeing extreme convergence under any truncation of the series.
    • §5Explicit Step Evaluations ($n=1$, $n=2$) — Numerically Verified ▸
  • Case $n=1$ (the Feller–Tornier bridge). $p=2$ is the sole saturated prime:
    $$C(1)=\zeta(2)\cdot\frac12\cdot\exp\left(\frac{103}{192}-2\ln\zeta(2)-\ln\zeta(4)-\frac83\ln\zeta(6)-\cdots\right)$$
  • Case $n=2$. $p=2$ and $p=3$ both collapse into the saturated domain, changing the leading coefficient to $\dfrac13$ and shifting the active-tail subtraction terms accordingly.

    §6Asymptotic Analysis & Connection to Sieve Theory ▸
  • Mertens' theorem convergence. As $n\to\infty$, $\Omega(n)$ stabilizes toward a bounded analytic limit, so the dominant asymptotic decay is driven entirely by the saturated harmonic product:
    $$D(n)\sim \frac{e^{-\gamma}}{\ln(n+1)}$$
  • Mapping to the twin-prime constant $C_2$. The active-tail equations intersect with the local prime-sifting obstructions found in Hardy–Littlewood-type limits.
    • §7Numerical Verification & Algorithmic Proof ▸
    Empirical convergence between raw sieve simulation and the higher-order Riemann zeta expansion is shown in §5 for $n=1$. The same procedure generalizes directly: for any $n$, sieve $D(n)$ over primes up to a large bound, then compare against the truncated zeta series with the saturated coefficient $\prod_{p\le n+1}(1-1/p)$.
    §8Conclusion & Open Inquiries ▸
    The saturated–active partition exhibits a fragmented family of constants — the Feller–Tornier constant ($n=1$), its higher-$n$ analogues, and the lift-survival constant $C(1)$ — as one continuous ladder indexed by block length $n$, with every term expressible purely in Riemann zeta values. Open directions include mapping the saturation step-function directly onto the error-term behavior of the Selberg sieve, and extending the closed form beyond $n=2$ to a fully general $k$-th saturated-coefficient formula.
