§1From corners to a spectrum

On the continuous curve C(t), the derivative has a sharp corner at each t = p − 1, and the corner's size is exactly 1 / p(p−1). Collect those corners into a measure — a spike of weight 1/p(p−1) at each prime location — and take its Fourier transform. The result is the oscillatory prime sum F(ξ) above.

F(ξ) is a wave built from every prime. As ξ varies, the terms ei(p−1)ξ rotate. At most values of ξ they point in scattered directions and partly cancel. But at special frequencies they line up — and where they line up is governed by how the primes sit in residue classes. The spectrum above is that wave; the controls below shape it.

Hover to read |F(ξ)|. The tall peak at ξ = 2π is where every term aligns; the dips and bumps at 2π/k carry the residue-class structure.
Note: weights are 1/p(p−1), so small primes dominate — with only a handful of primes the shape is already set, and adding thousands more barely changes it. Slide low to watch the spectrum build from the first few primes.
§2The resonances read the primes mod k

At ξ = 2π/k, the phase of each term depends only on (p−1) mod k. Since primes avoid every residue class that shares a factor with k, the sum at these points is shaped by that avoidance. What this is. F(ξ) genuinely responds to the distribution of primes in residue classes — the k = 2 suppression is the fact that all primes past 2 are odd; the k = 6 signature is that primes past 3 are ≡ 1 or 5 mod 6. These are visible because the transform encodes them, not because they are new. This is a lens on classical prime equidistribution (Dirichlet), drawn from the jump measure of C(t).
Honest note: this is a visualization, not a new result. The structure F(ξ) reveals — primes avoiding residue classes — is classical. What is shown here is that the saturation jumps of C(t), transformed, reconstruct that structure. The depth of oscillatory prime sums is real and well studied; nothing on this page claims to go beyond it.
