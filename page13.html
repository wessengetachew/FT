The Complex Extension

Pushing C(t) off the real line: a pole at s=1, a prefactor, a winding number, and zeros inherited from ζ(s) itself.
C(t,s) = ζ(s) · ∏p≤t+1(1−p1−s) · ∏p>t+1(1−(t+1)/ps)
ζ(s) the Riemann zeta function — supplies the only pole, at s=1.
∏p≤t+1(1−p1−s) the frozen primes: every prime up to t+1 has its local Euler factor capped, and each one vanishes exactly at s=1 (since p1−1=1), contributing a simple zero per prime.
∏p>t+1(1−(t+1)/ps) the tail: primes beyond t+1 keep their factor uncapped at the fixed value (t+1) rather than at p, and this infinite product is what carries the deformation as t varies continuously.
§1The Pole at s=1

Near s = 1, C(t,s) behaves like K(t)·(s−1)π(t+1)+t. Two mechanisms combine in that exponent: the frozen primes (p ≤ t+1) each contribute a clean factor of (s−1) since their local Euler factor vanishes exactly at s=1, giving the integer piece π(t+1); the tail of un-frozen primes diverges logarithmically as s→1 (the standard log ζ(s) ~ −log(s−1) behavior), contributing the continuous, generally-fractional piece t. Every kink in the real-axis curve C′(t) at t = p−1 is exactly an integer jump in this continuously-drifting exponent.

Step function π(t+1) (gold) plus the linear drift t (teal dashed) sum to the exponent (white curve). At small t each vertical line marks t = p−1 for a prime p; prime gridlines are hidden once t spans too wide a range to show them individually.
§2The Prefactor K(t)

The full asymptotic constant has a closed form built from three pieces: the frozen primes contribute ∏p≤t+1 log p; the tail's divergence, once the (s−1)t+1 piece is extracted, leaves a regularized constant e−(t+1)C₀e(t+1)Σp≤t+11/p; and the remaining tail product converges directly: ∏p>t+1(1−(t+1)/p)e(t+1)/p.

A real bug, caught by checking: the first derivation used Euler's constant γ ≈ 0.5772 in place of C₀. That's wrong — γ comes from ζ(s)'s own pole, but the prime zeta function P(s) = Σp p−s has a different constant from Möbius-inverting log ζ(s) = Σn P(ns)/n. Using γ produced a closed-form K(4.0) ≈ 4.29 against a numerically verified value of ≈ 372.5 — off by two orders of magnitude.
The fix: C₀ = Σn≥2 μ(n)/n · log ζ(n) ≈ −0.3157184519. With C₀ in place of γ, the closed form matches a high-precision numerical evaluation (using the prime zeta function directly for the divergent term, avoiding catastrophic cancellation) to 4–7 significant figures.

§3Branch Point & Monodromy

For non-integer t, the exponent π(t+1)+t is non-integer — so C(t,s) has a genuine branch point at s=1. Circling s=1 once picks up a phase of exactly 2π·(π(t+1)+t): tracking the unwrapped phase of C(t,s) around a small loop centered at s=1 matched the predicted value to 14+ digits (ratio 1.0000000000000002, for t=4, predicted 2π·7 ≈ 43.9823).

Left: unwrapped phase of C(t,s) as s circles s=1 (white) against the predicted slope 2π·(π(t+1)+t) (gold dashed). Center: the actual trajectory of C(t,s) in the complex plane over the same loop — it winds around the origin exactly that many times. Right: |C(t,s)| over the same loop, on a log scale — the magnitude swings by many orders as s passes nearest the pole/zeros. ζ(s) itself is now computed exactly (Euler–Maclaurin summation, machine precision out to |s−1|~0.8 — no longer the old local Laurent-series shortcut). One approximation remains: the tail-prime sum still uses its s→1 asymptotic form, so the winding count stays exactly right at any radius (it's a topological invariant, robust to small errors) while the precise shape/magnitude at large r carries a little residual approximation from that piece.
§4Zero Survival in the Strip

Because C(t,s) = ζ(s)·[everything else] by construction, every non-trivial zero of ζ(s) is automatically inherited by C(t,s) — unless the other factor happens to have a compensating pole at that exact point. That's the only non-trivial content here: checking the other factor stays finite. It's a structural fact more than a deep one, but it isn't free — a deformed, analytically-continued infinite product could in principle misbehave.

Checking this on the critical line required real work: the naive series expansion for the tail product diverges there (it only converges when pRe(s) > t+1, which fails for primes just above t+1 once Re(s) drops to 1/2). The fix splits the tail into a near band, evaluated by direct complex log with no series, and a far band, summed via the convergent prime-zeta series.
