§1One curve, sampled at the integers

Feller–Tornier, C(2), C(3), … are usually computed one at a time, as isolated constants. They are not isolated: they are values of a single continuous function C(t), obtained by letting the block length become a real variable t. Each prime's local factor is a smooth ramp (1 − (t+1)/p²) that flattens into a constant plateau (1 − 1/p) once t + 1 ≥ p. The result is continuous everywhere, smooth between events, with a corner at each t = p − 1.

Hover the curve to read C(t). Red dots are the integer constants; corners sit at t = p − 1.
§2The derivative reads off the primes

Differentiate. Between events the log-derivative is smooth, driven only by the still-active primes. At each t = p − 1 the prime p stops contributing — its factor goes flat — and the derivative jumps down by exactly:

Δ  =  1 / p(p−1)   at   t = p − 1

So the primes are literally readable off the steps of d/dt log C(t): each downward step marks a prime, and the step height pins down which prime. This is the Saturation Jump Theorem in its cleanest form — not a table of discrete values, but a visible structural feature of one continuous curve.

Each teal dot is the slope just before a prime saturates; each coral dot just after. The gap between them is the jump 1/p(p−1). Drag tmax above to follow the steps outward.
Why it's exact. Just below t = p − 1 only prime p's factor is changing in the relevant way; its ramp (1 − (t+1)/p²) contributes slope −1/(p² − (t+1)) to the log-derivative. At t = p − 1 that is −1/(p² − p) = −1/(p(p−1)). Just above, the factor is the constant (1 − 1/p), contributing nothing. The jump is the difference: exactly 1/(p(p−1)). Since p² − p = p(p−1) identically, there is no approximation anywhere.

Computed directly on the continuous curve (dense real t, numerical and exact): corners land at t = p − 1, the jumps match 1/p(p−1), and the curve is smooth in between.
